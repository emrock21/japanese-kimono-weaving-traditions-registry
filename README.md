# Japanese Kimono Weaving Traditions Registry

This repository preserves, in an immutable and decentralized format, the weaving traditions that form the textile foundation of the Japanese kimono. These techniques — tsumugi, kasuri, chirimen, jōfu, bashōfu, and others — represent centuries of regional craftsmanship, ecological adaptation, and aesthetic philosophy.

Many of these weaving traditions were endangered during the Meiji industrialization and the postwar economic shift. Documenting them on-chain ensures that their technical, cultural, and regional identities remain accessible for future generations.

## Why Japanese Weaving Traditions Matter

Kimono weaving is:

- a **regional identity system**, with each prefecture developing its own textile grammar  
- a **technical lineage**, transmitted through guilds and family workshops  
- a **material philosophy**, balancing silk, ramie, cotton, and banana fiber  
- a **cultural archive**, encoding motifs, climate adaptation, and social meaning  
- a **survivor of industrial suppression**, preserved by artisans against mass production  

Techniques like Yuki Tsumugi and Echigo Jōfu are recognized as **UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage**.

## Purpose of This Contract

The goal of this registry is to:

1. **Create a permanent, censorship-resistant record** of Japanese weaving traditions.  
2. **Preserve endangered textile knowledge** with technical precision.  
3. **Document materials, techniques, motifs, and cultural context** in structured form.  
4. **Provide an immutable reference** for museums, researchers, artisans, and future generations.  
5. **Honor the regional diversity of Japanese textile craftsmanship.**

The contract is intentionally:

- **ownerless** — no authority can alter or censor the registry  
- **payment-free** — no monetization, no barriers  
- **minimalistic and secure** — no external calls  
- **permanent** — entries become part of the cultural ledger  

## What Each Entry Records

- **textileName** — tsumugi, kasuri, chirimen, bashōfu, jōfu  
- **region** — Kyoto, Okinawa, Niigata, Ibaraki, Yonezawa  
- **materials** — silk, ramie, cotton, banana fiber  
- **techniques** — kasuri resist, twist weaving, snow bleaching, handloom  
- **motifs** — geometric, blurred kasuri, regional patterns  
- **culturalContext** — kimono types, seasonal use, ritual use  
- **uniqueness** — what distinguishes the textile
